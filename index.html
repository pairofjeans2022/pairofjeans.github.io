Land at Narita or Haneda. Allow 60–90 min for customs and baggage. Pick up Suica IC cards and SIM cards at the airport.

Take train to Nakano. From Narita: Narita Express to Shinjuku, transfer to Chuo Line (~80 min). From Haneda: Keikyu Line to Shinjuku, Chuo Line (~50 min). Check in and drop bags.

Notes
Keep Day 1 extremely low-key. Jet lag from New York to Tokyo (~14 hr time difference) hits hard. The goal is to land, orient, eat something real, and sleep at a reasonable hour so Day 2 isn't ruined.
Nakano Broadway closes ~8pm. If the group is too tired, skip it entirely — it's here all week.

Tonight's mission — three stops before bed:

1. 7-Eleven — Japan's 7-Eleven is genuinely one of the best things about the country. Not a regular convenience store. Stock up tonight for tomorrow's Sabbath picnic: onigiri (look for kombu or pickled plum for the vegan), inari sushi packs, edamame, tamagoyaki egg rolls, karaage chicken, melon bread, canned matcha or Boss coffee. Also grab tomorrow's breakfast. The quality is legitimately good — this is a highlight of the trip, not a backup plan.

2. Daiso dollar store — there's almost always one near a major station. Pick up a blue tarp (~$1 / ¥110) for the hanami picnic tomorrow morning. They also sell wet wipes, chopsticks, and small zip-lock bags (useful for phones at teamLab on Day 3).

3. Trash can note: Tokyo has almost no public trash cans — a legacy of post-1995 security measures. You will carry your wrappers and cups for a long time before finding a bin. The konbini you bought from will usually take back your trash, and parks have bins near the entrance. Pack a small bag inside your bag tomorrow as a portable trash holder. This catches everyone off guard on day one.

Schedule
9:00am
Head to Asakusa first. The kimono rental shop opens around 9–9:30am — go straight there. Getting 6 people dressed takes 30–45 min including hair and obi sash. Rental is fully paid in advance, so no transactions on the day.
10:00am
Walk out dressed and go straight to Kaminarimon Gate (Thunder Gate) — the iconic red lantern shot. This is your money photo location, right outside the rental shop. Hit it before midday crowds pack in.
10:30am
Walk the full Nakamise arcade in kimono, through to the Senso-ji main hall. Wave the incense smoke over yourself for good luck. Then Hozomon inner gate and the main forecourt.
11:30am
Asakusa Jinja shrine next door — quieter than Senso-ji, beautiful stone lanterns and a torii gate. Great for photos without crowds. Then Azumabashi bridge for the Skytree-and-kimono skyline shot.
12:30pm
Lunch in Asakusa in kimono — a full experience. Then take the Ginza Line from Asakusa to Ueno (2 stops, ~5 min).
2:00pm
Ueno Park hanami picnic — lay out the tarp, unpack the konbini supplies. Cherry blossoms in the afternoon light with everyone still in kimono. Walk Shinobazu Pond after — blossom reflections in the water, Bentendo Temple on the island. No rush to leave — stay until 4–5pm and soak it in properly.
4:30pm
Return kimonos to the Asakusa shop (most allow return up to 6pm). Sabbath observers head back to Nakano for a quiet evening. Non-observers: from Asakusa, get to Shinjuku (~20 min by train) for the JDM tour pickup — see evening option below.
5:00–6:00pm — Optional JDM evening (non-observers)
Pickup from Shinjuku area. Tours run ~3–4 hours through Tokyo and Yokohama's car culture circuit — C1 inner loop highway, Rainbow Bridge, APIT Autobacs (Japan's legendary car parts megastore), and Daikoku Parking Area, the real-life Fast and Furious car meet where modified GTRs, Supras, RX-7s, and supercars gather on weekend nights. Best experienced on a Saturday night when turnout is highest — April 4 is a Saturday, perfect timing.

Book one of these options:
— Daikoku PA + Nissan Facility Tour (GetYourGuide) ↗ — includes Nissan HQ visit, Yokohama guide, ~3 hrs, meets at Shin-Yokohama Station
— Legendary JDM Underground Meetup — Tokyo pickup ↗ — C1 highway, Rainbow Bridge, APIT, Daikoku, ~4 hrs
— Ride in a GTR R35/R34 to Daikoku ↗ — same circuit but you're riding inside an actual modified Nissan GTR. Premium option.

Heads up: Daikoku PA is technically a highway rest stop — police occasionally move people on, especially on busy nights. Turnout varies. Most reviews say Saturday nights are electric; weeknights more chill. April 4 is Saturday — good odds. Tours typically return to the pickup point by 9–10pm.
Sabbath notes
Kimono rental is fully paid in advance — no transactions on the day. Everything is free: Senso-ji (free), Ueno Park (free), Nakamise window browsing only. The only spend is the IC card tap for the Ginza Line Asakusa → Ueno, which a non-observer can handle.
Best photo spots in order: Kaminarimon gate → Nakamise alley → Hozomon inner gate → main hall forecourt → Asakusa Jinja → Azumabashi bridge → Ueno Park under the blossoms.
Chill Saturday evening alternative — Akihabara car scene: If the JDM tour feels like too much after a full day, Akihabara on a Saturday evening is a low-key car spot worth knowing about. Modified builds — GTRs, Supras, kei cars — park outside Yodobashi Camera and along the main strip most weekend nights. It's free, spontaneous, no booking, and pairs well with a walk through the lit-up Akihabara electronics district and a late ramen. Zero pressure, genuinely cool.
Trash can reminder: Pack your konbini picnic items in a bag with a small empty bag inside for rubbish. Ueno Park has bins near the main entrance and food stall areas, but they fill up fast on peak blossom days. Don't count on finding one mid-park.

Schedule
Morning — slow start
No alarm. Eat breakfast near the accommodation at your own pace — Nakano has good kissaten (old-school Japanese coffee shops) if you want a proper sit-down, or grab konbini and eat on the go.
11:00am
Head to Shimokitazawa — two stops from Nakano on the Keio Inokashira Line, about 10 min. One of Tokyo's best neighbourhood wanders. No agenda, no must-sees. The vibe is indie record stores, tiny live music venues, vintage clothing stacked floor to ceiling, hand-painted signage, cats in windows. It genuinely doesn't feel like Tokyo.
11:00am–3:00pm
Wander at will. Key pockets: the area around the south exit of the station is denser with vintage shops and cafés. Disk Union (used records) is a must for anyone into music. The backstreets north of the station are quieter and equally good. Don't plan this part — just walk.
1:00pm
Lunch in Shimokitazawa — the neighbourhood has solid options for every diet. Plenty of indie cafés serving proper food, not just coffee. Take your time.
3:00pm
Head back to Nakano. Rest, shower, and get ready. The kart tour picks up in Shinjuku area in the evening — you want to be fresh for it. This is intentional downtime before a big night.
6:00–7:00pm
Meet the kart operator in Shinjuku. Get suited up — helmet, gloves, costume. The evening start is deliberate: Tokyo at night with neon, street lights, and city glow from the kart is a completely different experience from daytime. You're driving through a video game. Most routes hit Shinjuku, Akihabara, and the waterfront areas — 2–3 hours on the road.
9:00–10:00pm
Tour ends back near Shinjuku. Group is wired — go to Golden Gai (200 tiny bars a 10-min walk from Shinjuku Station, each holding 6–8 people). Split into pairs and try different bars, meet back outside. Or head to Omoide Yokocho for smoky yakitori and highballs to wind down. Late night is yours.
Karting — book in advance
Request an evening departure slot when booking — most operators offer 5pm, 6pm, or 7pm starts. Night tours often cost slightly more but reviews consistently rate them higher for atmosphere. All participants need a valid driver's license.

Booking options:
— Street Kart Tokyo — Klook ↗ ~$40–55 pp (¥6,000–8,000), Shinjuku pickup
— Tokyo Go-Kart Tour — GetYourGuide ↗ multiple time slots including evening
— Tokyo Go-Kart — Viator ↗ group booking option for 6

Note: Operators change frequently in Tokyo due to licensing. Verify availability for Apr 7 on each platform and check recent reviews before confirming.

Mt. Fuji is not climbable in April — climbing season opens July 1. The day trip is about the views, the lake, and the surrounding area. Visibility is the main variable — Apr 8 is historically a clearer week in spring.

Schedule
9:30am
Head to Shinjuku Station. On Saturdays the Saphir Odoriko departs from Shinjuku — perfect since you're based in Nakano (5 min on the Chuo Line). Grab breakfast at the station or a konbini on the way.
~10:00am
Board the Saphir Odoriko. Deep blue luxury limited express — all Green Car seating, skylights, and a cafeteria on board. The train hugs the Pacific coastline from Atami onward. On clear days you get views of Mt. Fuji from the right side of the train. Book seats in Cars 5–8 for the standard Green Car, or Cars 2–3 for a private group compartment (fits up to 6, around a table, perfect).
~12:30pm
Arrive at Kawazu Station. Take the Tokai Bus C50 toward Shuzenji (~25 min, ~$5 / ¥730) to the Kawazu Nanadaru Onsen stop at the trailhead.
1:00–3:00pm
Walk the Kawazu Seven Waterfalls trail — 1.3km one-way through a forested gorge, seven distinct waterfalls each named and featuring a Lucky God statue. The trail ends at Odaru Falls, the largest at 30m. Wear shoes with good grip — the mossy stone steps are slippery when wet. Allow 2 hours at a relaxed pace.
3:00–5:00pm
Amagiso Odaru Onsen at the base of Odaru Falls — one of the most unique onsen in Japan. Mixed-gender open-air baths right at the foot of a 30m waterfall. Swimsuits required (bring your own or rent at reception). Completely tattoo-friendly — no restrictions, no stickers needed. Multiple rotenburo (outdoor baths) along the river, a hot spring lap pool downstream, and a hidden cave onsen to explore. Entry ~$7 pp (¥1,000).
~5:30pm
Bus back to Kawazu Station. Optional: walk the wasabi fields just past Odaru Falls before leaving — fresh wasabi growing in clear mountain stream water, one of the most unusual sights in Japan.
~6:00pm
Board the return Odoriko or Saphir Odoriko back to Tokyo (~2.5 hrs). Arrive Shinjuku ~8:30pm. Final group dinner near Nakano or Shinjuku — the last big night.
Train + booking
Saphir Odoriko — book in advance:
Saturday departures leave from Shinjuku. The train runs once or twice daily — seats sell out, especially on Saturdays in spring. Book as early as possible.

— Book via Klook ↗ ~$25–63 pp depending on class
— JR East official site ↗ — book the private compartment (Cars 2–3) here, fits 4–6 people around a table

Private compartment for 6: Cars 2–3 have compartments for up to 6 people — a shared window, leather seats, wooden table. Perfect for the group. Must be booked at a JR Ticket Office (not online). Worth doing in person or via a travel agent.
Amagiso Odaru Onsen: ~$7 pp (¥1,000) entry. Bring your swimsuit — no nude bathing in the outdoor baths. Towel rental available (~$1 / ¥100). Open Thursday–Tuesday, 10am–4pm. Closed Wednesdays. No booking needed — walk in. Note: the path down to the onsen is steep and slippery. Wear appropriate footwear for the trail.
Total trip cost estimate per person: Train round trip ~$50–130 + bus ~$10 + onsen ~$7 = roughly $67–147 pp for the whole day. One of the most unique days on the itinerary and one of the cheapest.
Food on the day
On the train
Saphir Odoriko cafeteria car — Izu shrimp noodle soup, local black beef dishes, wasabi cheese snacks, desserts. Book ahead via the Saphir Odoriko app. Limited availability — order as soon as you board
Vegan / veg
Pack konbini supplies from Nakano the night before — the Kawazu trail area has limited vegan options. Inari sushi, onigiri (kombu filling), and fruit are reliable. The wasabi fields sell fresh wasabi products including wasabi-flavored snacks
Pesc / meat
Kawazu town near the station has seafood restaurants and soba spots — good for a quick lunch before the bus. Grilled river fish (ayu) is the local specialty in spring
Final dinner (all)
Back in Shinjuku or Nakano — make it a proper last-night izakaya. Wagyu yakiniku works for the whole group: meat eaters get wagyu, vegans/veg get mushrooms and vegetables on the grill, everyone shares the table
Why this day works: The Saphir Odoriko is one of Japan's finest train journeys — sapphire blue Pacific coastline, possible Fuji views, and a cafeteria on board. The waterfalls trail is one hour of easy forest walking. And Amagiso is one of the only truly tattoo-friendly, mixed-gender, open-air waterfall onsens in Japan — genuinely rare. For ~$7 entry next to a 30m waterfall in a swimsuit, it's one of the most memorable things you'll do on the trip.

02
Food guide — eating as a mixed group
Strategy
Go for izakaya
Izakayas serve dozens of small shared plates — yakitori for meat eaters, agedashi tofu and grilled veg for vegetarians, sashimi for the pescetarian. One table, everyone's covered. Look for ones with a photo menu.
Watch out for
Hidden dashi
Traditional Japanese cooking uses dashi (fish stock) as a base in miso soup, noodle broths, and sauces. Dishes that look vegetarian often aren't. Ask: 「だしは何ですか？」 (What is the dashi made from?)
For vegans + vegetarians
Safe bets
Inari sushi (tofu pockets), onigiri with kombu or pickled plum filling, zaru soba, vegetable tempura at vegan-specific spots, and T's TanTan in Tokyo Station — fully vegan ramen.
For the pescetarian
Spoiled for choice
Conveyor belt sushi (kaiten-zushi), kaisendon sashimi bowls, grilled fish teishoku set meals, prawn tempura. Toyosu Market on Day 3 is the highlight — fresh uni, tuna, salmon at the source.
For meat eaters
Don't miss
Wagyu yakiniku, tonkatsu, tonkotsu ramen, yakitori in Omoide Yokocho. Nakano has excellent yakitori alleys 5 min from the accommodation.
Must-try for everyone
Konbini culture
7-Eleven, FamilyMart, and Lawson are a food experience. Onigiri, egg salad sandwiches, melon bread, cold matcha. Budget one full konbini breakfast as a group activity — it's part of the trip.
